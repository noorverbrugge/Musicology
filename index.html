Computational musicology uses digital tools and data analysis to study musical structure, style, and performance. In this portfolio, I explore a small class corpus consisting of songs by Bruno Mars and Michael Jackson. These artists were selected because they both operate within mainstream pop, but differ in production style and historical context, making them suitable for comparison.

I analyse metadata such as tempo, danceability, energy, valence, and loudness, as well as audio-derived features such as chromagrams, timbre representations, and self-similarity matrices. The goal is to demonstrate how computational methods can reveal musical patterns that are not immediately apparent through listening alone. In particular, I focus on structure, repetition, harmony, timbre, and tempo. Together, these visualisations provide a more detailed understanding of how songs are organised over time.

Corpus overview

The corpus consists of a small selection of tracks by Bruno Mars and Michael Jackson, obtained from the class dataset. The tracks include a mix of songs from different periods of each artist’s career, which allows for a broader comparison of their musical styles.

However, the corpus is relatively small and may not fully represent the complete stylistic range of either artist. As a result, the findings should be interpreted as exploratory rather than definitive. The selection of tracks may also introduce bias, depending on which songs are included.

This section gives a general overview of the class corpus. I combine my Bruno Mars and Michael Jackson track lists into one dataset so that I can compare the two groups visually. Analysis

The scatterplot shows that both artists occupy a similar pop-oriented feature space, with generally moderate to high values for danceability and energy. This reflects their shared focus on rhythm, groove, and accessibility within mainstream pop music.

At the same time, there are subtle but meaningful differences. Michael Jackson’s tracks tend to reach higher energy values and show a wider spread across the feature space, while Bruno Mars’ tracks appear more tightly clustered. This suggests that Michael Jackson’s music exhibits greater variation in intensity, whereas Bruno Mars’ tracks are more consistent in their overall energy profile.

These patterns indicate that while the two artists overlap stylistically, there may still be enough variation for computational models to detect differences, although not perfectly.

This histogram shows the distribution of tempo values in the class corpus. Each bar represents how many tracks fall within a certain BPM range. This helps us see whether the songs tend to cluster around certain tempo ranges.

Analysis

The histogram shows a clear concentration of tracks around 110–130 BPM, which is typical for mainstream pop music. Both artists exhibit substantial overlap in their tempo distributions, suggesting that tempo alone is not a strong distinguishing feature.

However, there are subtle differences. Bruno Mars’ tracks show slightly more variation at higher tempo values, while Michael Jackson’s tracks are more concentrated around the central tempo range. This suggests that although both artists operate within similar tempo conventions, Bruno Mars may explore a slightly wider range of tempi across songs. This confirms that tempo is a shared stylistic constraint within pop music rather than a distinguishing feature between artists.

A tempo curve shows how the estimated tempo changes over time in a track. Unlike the Spotify tempo value, which gives only one average BPM value, a tempo curve can reveal small fluctuations or expressive timing differences during the song.

Analysis

This visualisation does not show tempo changes within a single track, but instead compares the tempo of different tracks. The values vary considerably, ranging from slower ballads to faster tracks. This indicates that Bruno Mars uses a range of tempo values across songs, rather than maintaining a single tempo style.

This reinforces the idea that tempo is a descriptive feature of individual tracks, but not necessarily a stable characteristic of an artist’s overall style.

The chromagram clearly shows harmonic repetition and confirms that the song has a strong and stable tonal focus. Compared to more harmonically complex genres, the pitch activity is concentrated around a limited group of notes. This supports the song’s clear pop style and strong tonal identity. The visual repetition also helps make the formal structure visible, because similar harmonic material tends to return in similar sections. Analysis

The timbre visualisation shows very little variation across frequency bins, with most values appearing similar. While this could suggest a consistent production style, it may also be the result of scaling issues in the visualisation.

Because of this lack of contrast, it is difficult to draw strong conclusions about timbral variation from this plot alone. This highlights an important limitation: not all visualisations equally reveal meaningful musical structure, and careful interpretation is required.

The chroma-based self-similarity matrix shows clear block-like shapes along the main diagonal. These square or rectangular areas represent repeated harmonic sections, such as verses and choruses. The strong contrast between similar and different regions shows that harmony plays an important role in shaping the overall structure of the song. The repeating diagonal patterns reflect stable chord progressions and a consistent tonal centre.

In contrast, the timbre-based self-similarity matrix looks more uniform and dense. Although some structural boundaries are still visible, the contrast between sections is less strong. This suggests that while the harmony clearly repeats, the timbral characteristics, such as instrumentation, texture, and production details, remain relatively stable throughout the song.

Analysis

The model achieves an accuracy of approximately 0.43, which is relatively low and close to random guessing. This suggests that the selected features are not sufficient to reliably distinguish between the two artists.

This result is consistent with earlier visualisations, which showed a strong overlap between the feature distributions of Bruno Mars and Michael Jackson. In other words, the stylistic similarity between the artists makes classification based on these high-level features difficult.

These results should be interpreted with caution. The dataset is relatively small, and the features used are quite general. More detailed features, such as timbre descriptors or temporal structure, or a larger dataset, could potentially improve classification performance.

Conclusion

This portfolio explored the musical characteristics of Bruno Mars and Michael Jackson using a combination of metadata features, audio representations, and machine learning techniques.

The results show that both artists share many similarities in terms of tempo, energy, and danceability, reflecting their position within mainstream pop music. This overlap was visible in multiple visualisations, including the scatterplot and tempo distribution, and was confirmed by the relatively low classification accuracy.

At the same time, some subtle differences were observed. For example, Michael Jackson’s tracks showed a wider spread in energy values, while Bruno Mars’ tracks appeared more consistent. These differences suggest that although the artists operate within a similar stylistic space, there are still measurable variations in their musical characteristics.

The chromagram and self-similarity analyses highlighted the importance of harmonic structure, showing clear repetition and a stable tonal centre. In contrast, the timbre-based representations showed less variation, suggesting that production style remains relatively consistent throughout the track.

Overall, this analysis demonstrates that computational methods can provide useful insights into musical structure and style. However, the results are limited by the size of the dataset and the simplicity of the features used. Future work could include larger datasets and more detailed audio features to gain a deeper understanding of stylistic differences between artists.

Assumptions and Limitations

This analysis assumes that Spotify features such as danceability, energy, and valence are reliable representations of musical characteristics. It also assumes that the selected tracks are representative of each artist’s style.

However, these assumptions may not always hold. These features are simplified abstractions and may not capture the full musical complexity of a track. Additionally, the dataset is relatively small, which limits the generalisability of the conclusions.

Finally, some visualisations, particularly those based on timbre, may be affected by scaling or preprocessing choices, which can influence how patterns appear. This means that conclusions should be interpreted carefully and in context.
